Yes — that is a valid and often better second-pass method.

Your original file mostly tells you:

which brands are being mentioned

in what contexts

with what repeated descriptive fragments

But it does not fully expose the model’s synthesized attribute view of each brand.

So after you identify the top brands from prompt-output extraction, it can be smart to run a focused attribute-elicitation pass on those brands.

The key correction is this:

Do not ask the model “how do you know?” as the main extraction prompt.
Ask it to first fill the attribute structure, and only then ask why each filled attribute was assigned.

That sequence is much stronger.

Why this is better

If you directly ask:

How do you know Brand X is premium / trustworthy / broad-service / etc.?

the model may start inventing explanations.

But if you first force it to commit to a structured table like:

category

location

core services

wedge

trust markers

price tier

operating model

likely competitors

likely target customer

and then ask:

which of these are explicit?

which are inferred?

which are uncertain?

what signals caused each one?

then you get a much cleaner read.

That is closer to:
what attribute map the LLM has formed about the brand.

The better framework
Pass 1: observed mention extraction

You already did this.

That gives:

top brands

repeated descriptors

competitor co-mentions

raw signal fragments

Pass 2: structured brand-attribute elicitation

Now query the LLM on each top brand and ask it to fill a predefined schema.

This is useful because LLMs often hold a more complete “compressed brand representation” than what appears in one-off raw mentions.

Pass 3: evidence tagging

For every attribute, ask the model to tag:

explicitly stated

likely inferred

uncertain

generic category assumption

competitor-relative inference

This is the most important part.

Pass 4: causal explanation

Then ask:

what likely signals led you to this attribute?

what wording / source pattern would make this stronger?

what competing brands occupy similar position?

what is the strongest differentiating attribute?

That gives you “how the model knows” in a more usable way.

So your idea is good, but the prompt shape matters

Instead of:

Fill the table and tell me how you know

use:

Step A

“Fill the table.”

Step B

“For each field, classify whether this is:

explicit,

inferred,

weakly inferred,

uncertain.”

Step C

“For each field, state the top reasons/signals that likely caused this assignment.”

Step D

“What competing brands have a similar attribute pattern?”

That is the right order.

Why this helps versus your current extraction

Your current extraction is mostly surface evidence.

This next pass helps reveal:

how the LLM internally clusters the brand

which attributes are durable in its representation

which ones are vague or hallucinated

which competitor the brand “feels like” in model space

That is valuable because a brand may be mentioned often but still have a weak identity.

Example:

many mentions of “quality home healthcare”

but weak association with any unique service, audience, or trust marker

In that case, mention count is high, but brand formation is weak.

Your second-pass prompt helps expose that.

The main risk

The danger is that the model will over-complete missing fields.

For example, for a healthcare brand it may automatically infer:

premium

licensed

24/7

personalized

patient-centric

simply because that is category-typical language.

So you need the prompt to penalize generic category assumptions.

Add a rule like:

Do not assign an attribute just because it is common in the category. Mark it as “generic category prior” unless there is brand-specific evidence or repeated strong association.

That improves quality a lot.

Best prompt design

Use a schema like this for each brand:

| Attribute | Filled value | Confidence 0-100 | Explicit / inferred / uncertain | Why assigned | Likely source of signal | Generic or distinctive? |

And the attributes can be:

category

geography

service set

primary use case

target customer

trust markers

speed / availability

price tier

brand wedge

premium vs mass

incumbent vs modern specialist

competitor neighborhood

strongest remembered trait

weakest / missing trait

This is much more useful than a simple brand summary.

Better question than “how do you know?”

A better question is:

What signals most likely caused you to associate this brand with this attribute?

Because “how do you know” invites fake certainty.

“Signals most likely caused” is softer and closer to what you want.

Even better:

Is this attribute based on explicit recurring evidence, broad category prior, competitor comparison, or inferred positioning?

That is very strong.

What this gives you for the brand you are testing

Now you can compare:

competitor attribute map

What the model thinks about each top brand

versus

target brand attribute map

What the model thinks about your client brand

Then you can see:

missing attributes

weak attributes

wrong attributes

over-generic attributes

wedges competitors own but your brand does not

trust markers competitors have that your brand lacks

where your brand is not entering the right competitor neighborhood

That is far better than just “who got mentioned.”

The strongest use of this method

This works especially well for attributes like:

wedge

premium vs affordable

modern vs incumbent

broad-service vs specialist

urgent-care vs preventive-care

family-focused vs professional-focused

trust perception

competitor neighborhood

Those are often emergent model impressions, and your second-pass prompting can reveal them.

What I would not trust too much from this prompt

Still be cautious with:

founding year

exact pricing

exact coverage areas

operational hours

licensing specifics

exact service catalog

For those, prompt-based elicitation can still overfill.

The right overall answer

So yes, after getting top brands from prompt extraction, it is a good idea to run a focused prompt that:

fills a predefined attribute structure

tags each field as explicit vs inferred vs uncertain

explains the likely signals behind each field

identifies whether the attribute is distinctive or just category-generic

compares the brand’s attribute map to your test brand

That is a better way to understand what attribute identity the LLM has formed.

Best practical setup

Use both:

Layer 1

Observed prompt-output extraction
This tells you what repeatedly surfaced.

Layer 2

Structured attribute elicitation
This tells you what the model has compressed into its brand representation.

Layer 3

Evidence-type tagging
This tells you whether those attributes are grounded, inferred, generic, or unstable.

That combination is much stronger than either one alone.
