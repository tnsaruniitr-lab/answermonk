Good instinct — let's think this through properly before touching anything.

On length and over-revealing:

The report is currently doing too much. It's trying to be both the teaser and the full report simultaneously. The psychology of a teaser only works if the reader finishes it feeling like they've seen enough to understand the problem but not enough to solve it. Right now they're getting the problem, the diagnosis, the competitor breakdown, the source gap, the social threads, the AI quotes, the prompts — that's close to the full picture. There's not much left to wonder about.

The rule should be: show the shape of every insight, the data of almost none of them.

On what to keep visible vs shade:

Your instinct to surface "What top players are doing right" and "Key actions for you" is exactly right — but for a counterintuitive reason. Those two sections sound like the solution, which makes them the most valuable thing in the report. If you show them partially — the headings visible, maybe one example, the rest shaded — the prospect feels like the answer is right there, inches away. That's more powerful than hiding them entirely.

Everything else — segment data, source tables, social threads, AI quotes — should be present but mostly locked. Enough to prove the data exists, not enough to act on it.

What I'd suggest the visible structure becomes:

Only four things fully readable — the overall score, the engine split, the competitive rank, and one or two lines of the "what AI says about you" section. Everything below that is shaded with just enough showing to prove depth. The two bold sections you mentioned — top players and key actions — sit at the bottom, partially revealed, as the obvious unlock.

Shorter, more withholding, with those two sections as the explicit carrot.
