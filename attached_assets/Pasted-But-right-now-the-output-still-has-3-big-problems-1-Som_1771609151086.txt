1) Some numbers are still logically impossible

Example:

83% category match, 0% audience match
Snippet: “...for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)”

That is a direct audience match for SMBs or startups-adjacent.
So your audience classifier is still too literal.

What’s happening

Your audience dictionary is probably:

startup, startups, early-stage

But real sources say:

SMEs

businesses

companies

founders

growing companies

finance teams

emerging businesses

LLMs consider those semantically equivalent.
Your system doesn’t → therefore conclusions look arbitrary.

Fix (critical)

Switch audience scoring from keyword matching → semantic bucket matching.

Do NOT use embeddings. Keep it simple rule-based:

Audience buckets
SMB bucket

SME, SMB, small business, small and medium enterprises, businesses, companies, finance teams, growing companies

Startup bucket

startup, startups, early-stage, founders, seed, venture-backed, scaleups

Now score like this:

explicit match = 2

adjacent match = 1

none = 0

Then compute % of snippets with ≥1.

That alone will remove most “0% but clearly present” credibility breaks.

2) Authority is currently misleading (and hurting trust)

You’re showing:

Not found on any third-party supporting sources

But you literally cite funding/press snippets in the evidence panel in other segments.

The problem:
You’re computing authority per segment independently, but retrieval is shared across prompts.

So the same Forbes/VC article appears in corporate_cards but not expense_management — even though the AI model absolutely uses it globally to understand the company.

Why this matters

Users think:

“This tool is hallucinating. I know we have press coverage.”

They don’t understand segment-scoped retrieval.

Fix — split authority into 2 layers
Segment Authority (keep)

Used for ranking explanation

Global Retrieval Authority (add)

Used for trust

Compute once across all segments:

unique high-tier domains mentioning brand

Show it at top:

Global model familiarity: High (mentioned across 7 authority domains)

Now segment authority becomes believable instead of contradictory.

3) Comparative Presence is half-correct but confusing

You currently treat any multi-brand surface equally:

Example:

Listed on Qashio LinkedIn (position 30)

This is technically true but useless insight.

To a user, LinkedIn ≠ comparison article.

Fix: classify comparison surfaces into 3 tiers
Tier A — decision surfaces (high impact)

Top/Best lists
Alternatives pages
Review platforms
Directories
Buyer guides

Tier B — contextual surfaces (medium impact)

News articles mentioning multiple brands
Industry reports
Funding round comparisons

Tier C — incidental co-mentions (low impact)

LinkedIn posts
Investor portfolios
Aggregators

Then compute:

Comparison Strength =
3 × TierA +
1 × TierB +
0.2 × TierC

And only show Tier A + B in UI.

Now the insight becomes meaningful.
