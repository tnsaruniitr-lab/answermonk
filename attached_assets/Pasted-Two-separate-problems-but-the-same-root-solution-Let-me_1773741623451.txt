Two separate problems but the same root solution. Let me untangle them.

Problem 1 — Competitive Analysis Category Drift
When you send 3 websites to Claude separately, it will naturally anchor each brand in its own strongest category. A generalist competitor might come back as "digital health platform" while your primary brand is "at-home healthcare." Now you're scoring them on different frameworks and the comparison is meaningless.
The fix: category is derived once, from the primary brand only.
Competitors never go through signal derivation. They inherit the primary brand's signal framework and are tested against it. The flow becomes:
Primary brand URL → Stage 1 → derives category + 4 signals + questions
Competitor URLs   → skip Stage 1 entirely
                  → tested with the same 4 questions as primary brand
                  → scored by Claude against the same signal definitions
The primary brand is the anchor. You're not asking "what is this competitor good at" — you're asking "how does this competitor perform on the dimensions that matter for my brand's category." That's the right competitive question anyway.

Problem 2 — Multi-Service / Multi-Product Brands
This is trickier. A brand like Valeo offers blood tests, IV therapy, doctor visits, physiotherapy. A SaaS might offer debt collection software, dunning, payment recovery. If you derive signals at the service level you get fragmentation — each service looks like a different business.
The fix: Business Type as the category anchor, services as question parameters.
You need a two-level taxonomy:
Business Type  →  the stable, unifying category level
               →  "At-home healthcare provider"
               →  "Revenue recovery SaaS"

Service Verticals  →  what varies per question
                   →  blood tests, IV therapy, doctor visits
                   →  debt collection, dunning, payment recovery
Signals are always derived at the Business Type level — things like clinical credibility, service accessibility, provider trust, geographic coverage apply to the whole business, not one service. But the question for each signal gets parameterized with the specific service.
So instead of one question per signal, you get question variants:
Signal: Service Accessibility
→ "How quickly can you get a blood test done at home in Dubai?"
→ "How quickly can you get IV therapy at home in Dubai?"
→ "How quickly can a doctor visit you at home in Dubai?"
Same signal, same scoring framework, different service surface. This also handles geo naturally — location just becomes another parameter in the question, not a separate framework.

How This Changes Your Stage 1 Prompt
Claude needs one additional instruction during signal derivation: derive at the business model level, not the service level. And it needs to return the service verticals separately so your engine knows how to parameterize questions.
The Stage 1 output schema should expand to:
json{
  "brand_name": "",
  "business_type": "",
  "service_verticals": [],
  "geo_markets": [],
  "segment": "",
  "signals": [
    {
      "id": "signal_1",
      "name": "",
      "why_it_matters_for_ai_visibility": "",
      "what_strong_looks_like": "",
      "what_weak_looks_like": "",
      "base_question": "",
      "question_variants": []
    }
  ]
}
```

`base_question` is the signal-level question. `question_variants` are the service and geo parameterized versions Claude auto-generates. Your engine then decides — run base question only for MVP, or expand to variants when the user opts in.

## The Unified Mental Model
```
Business Type    →  what defines the category (never changes per competitor)
Service Verticals →  what parameterizes the questions
Geo Markets      →  second parameter on top of service
Signals          →  always derived at Business Type level
Competitors      →  always inherit primary brand's signals, never re-derive
This way whether you're testing one brand or three, one service or six, one city or four — the scoring framework stays consistent and the numbers are actually comparable.
